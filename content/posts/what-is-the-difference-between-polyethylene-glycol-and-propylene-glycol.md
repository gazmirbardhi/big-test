Polyethylene glycol (PEG) and propylene glycol (PG) are both glycols with a wide range of applications. PEG is a water-soluble polymer that is often used as a surfactant, solvent, and lubricant, while PG is a clear, colorless, and slightly syrupy liquid that is used as an ingredient in many different products. It is important to understand the differences between these two substances, especially since they are often used interchangeably in some applications.

## Definition of Polyethylene Glycol (PEG)

Polyethylene glycol, also known as polyethylene oxide, is a synthetic polymer made from ethylene oxide. PEG is a water-soluble, non-ionic compound that is used in a wide range of applications due to its low toxicity, low volatility, and high water solubility. It is a viscous liquid at room temperature and is often used as a surfactant, solvent, and lubricant.

## Definition of Propylene Glycol (PG)

Propylene glycol, also known as 1,2-propanediol, is a clear, colorless, and slightly syrupy liquid that is used as an ingredient in many different products. It is a synthetic compound made from propylene oxide, and it is often used as a solvent, humectant, and preservative.

While PEG and PG are similar in some ways, they have different chemical structures and physical properties, which can affect their performance and safety in different applications.
